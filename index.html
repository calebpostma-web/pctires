Tire Education
The PC Tires Buying Guide

Not sure what you need? We'll walk you through everything — from season to sidewall markings.

🌤️
All-Season
The default tire that comes on most vehicles. Designed for year-round use in mild climates — dry, wet, and light snow. They compromise between summer grip and winter traction, so they're not the best at either. Fine if you rarely see heavy snow or temperatures below -7°C.
Good for: Mild Canadian winters, mostly highway driving
🌨️
All-Weather (3PMSF)
A newer category — essentially a better all-season. Certified for severe snow service (the mountain snowflake symbol ❄️). Significantly better than all-season in snow and ice, while still being driveable in summer. The best one-tire option for Ontario drivers who don't want to swap seasonally.
Best for: Most Ontario drivers wanting one set year-round
❄️
Winter / Snow Tires
Dedicated winter tires use a special rubber compound that stays soft and grippy below -7°C, where all-season rubber hardens. They have aggressive tread patterns to channel snow and slush. Dramatically shorter stopping distances on ice. Swap in October, swap back in April.
Best for: Maximum safety in Canadian winters
☀️
Summer / Performance
Maximum dry and wet grip in warm temperatures. Use a softer compound optimized for heat. Never drive these below 7°C — the rubber becomes dangerously brittle and stopping distances increase massively. For sports cars, performance vehicles, or enthusiast drivers.
Warning: Not for use below 7°C

Every tire has a code on its sidewall. Here's how to read 225/65R17 102H and other markings:

225
Section Width
Width of the tire in millimetres, measured from sidewall to sidewall.
/65
Aspect Ratio
Sidewall height as a percentage of the width. Lower number = shorter sidewall = sportier look and handling.
R
Radial Construction
How the internal cords are laid. Virtually all modern tires are radial — this will always be "R".
17
Rim Diameter
The wheel size in inches. This must match your rim exactly — a 17" tire only fits a 17" rim.
102
Load Index
Maximum weight the tire can carry. 102 = 850 kg per tire. Must meet or exceed vehicle manufacturer's requirement.
H
Speed Rating
Maximum sustained speed. H = 210 km/h. V = 240. W = 270. Y = 300. Always match or exceed your original rating.
OWL / RWL
Outlined / Raised White Letters
Decorative white lettering on the sidewall. Popular on trucks and SUVs for a rugged appearance. Purely cosmetic — no performance difference.
BSW
Black Sidewall
Standard black sidewall with no lettering. The most common type. Cleaner look for passenger cars and crossovers.
❄️ M+S
Winter Certification Symbols
M+S = Mud & Snow (basic). The mountain/snowflake symbol (3PMSF) is the stricter certification — required for dedicated winter or all-weather tires.

Tread pattern affects everything from handling to noise to water evacuation. Here are the four main types:

〓
Symmetric Tread
Same pattern across the whole tire. Can be rotated in any direction. Quietest ride, good all-round performance. Common on economy all-season tires.
⟹
Directional / V-Pattern
V-shaped grooves point in one direction — designed to pump water out at high speed. Excellent wet-weather performance. Must be mounted so the arrow on the sidewall points forward. Common on performance and winter tires.
◨
Asymmetric Tread
Different inner and outer tread zones — outer for dry cornering, inner for wet grip. High-performance tires. Must be mounted with the "outside" facing out. Cannot be rotated side-to-side.
🏔️
Aggressive / All-Terrain
Large, widely spaced lugs for off-road grip and mud self-cleaning. Common on trucks and SUVs. Louder on highway than passenger tires. Some wear faster on pavement.
Treadwear indicator: Look for the small raised bars in the tread grooves — when your tread is flush with these bars, you're at 2/32" (the legal minimum in Canada). We recommend replacing at 4/32" for safety, especially in winter. Use a toonie: if the silver band shows above the tread, it's time to replace.
🛣️
Highway (HT)
Smooth, quiet ride optimized for pavement. Great fuel economy. Low rolling resistance. Best for trucks and SUVs that stay on-road. Most comfortable for daily driving and highway trips.
Best for: Daily commuters, highway driving
🌲
All-Terrain (AT)
The middle ground. Handles pavement well, but adds capability for gravel, dirt, and light off-road use. Slightly louder than highway tires. Very popular for pickup trucks in Ontario — great for cottages and work sites.
Best for: Trucks that see both road and occasional off-road
🪨
Mud-Terrain (MT)
Aggressive, widely-spaced lugs for serious off-road traction in mud, rock, and sand. Very loud on highway. Wears faster on pavement. Not ideal for daily driving unless you regularly go off-road.
Not ideal for daily highway commuting
⚡
Load Range Matters
Trucks and vans often require Load Range C, D, or E tires — these have stiffer sidewalls to handle heavier loads and towing. Never downgrade your load rating. If you tow a trailer or haul loads, confirm your required load range before ordering.
Check before ordering if you tow or haul
Lifted trucks: If your truck is lifted, your tire size will be different from stock. Tell us your lift height and we'll find compatible sizes. Larger tires may also require a speedometer recalibration — ask us about this at installation.
